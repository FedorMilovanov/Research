## 1. New underdeveloped zone: the heart in prayer and after prayer

The archive has strong material on thought-life, temptation, self-deception, conscience, and mortification, but the **heart in prayer** is still comparatively thin. This matters because prayer exposes what the heart believes God is like, what it wants, what it fears, and what it does after asking.

### Thomas Goodwin — *The Return of Prayers*

**Use.** Add Goodwin as a core locator for a new module: *the heart after prayer*. The research should not only ask, “What does the heart do in temptation?” but also, “What does the heart do when it waits for God?”

**Source signal.** Online copies list chapters such as “Observations made upon the disposition of the heart after prayer,” “Considerations to quiet the heart,” and application against those who pray without watching for returns.

**Article use.** This strengthens a future section: *Prayer is not a religious escape from the heart; it is one of the places where the heart becomes visible.*

**Quote status.** `LOCATOR_PLUS_EXACT_QUOTE_CANDIDATE`. Before article use, open the PDF/page and extract short exact quotations only.

### Nathanael Vincent — *The Spirit of Prayer*

**Use.** This adds a pneumatological prayer layer: the Spirit gives life, liberty, holy motions, and affections; prayer is not merely verbal performance.

**Article use.** Helpful for guarding against two errors: prayer as technique and prayer as emotional self-generation.

**Quote status.** `LOCATOR_PLUS_EXACT_QUOTE_CANDIDATE`; Digital Puritan PDF is suitable for checking exact lines, but OCR must be verified.

## 2. New underdeveloped zone: faith and love set on Christ, then expressed in works

The archive has Edwards on holy practice, but it needs a pre-Edwards bridge that ties faith/love directly to Christ as object and to works as expression.

### John Preston — *The Breastplate of Faith and Love*

**Use.** Preston is valuable because the work is explicitly framed around faith and love as they are “set upon Christ their object” and “expressed in good works.” This gives a clean bridge between inner heart-movement and embodied practice.

**Article use.** Use Preston to avoid both inwardism and externalism:

- inwardism: endlessly studying the heart without love moving toward Christ and neighbor;
- externalism: measuring works without faith and love set on Christ.

**Quote status.** `PRIMARY_LOCATOR_AVAILABLE`; exact quotation requires opening page images / HathiTrust / EEBO-derived text.

### John Preston — *The Saints Daily Exercise*

**Use.** Adds the prayer/ordinary-discipline layer: the heart is formed not only in major crises but in daily exercise before God.

### Thomas Hooker — *The Application of Redemption*

**Use.** Hooker is valuable as a locator for conversion, preparation, the Word and Spirit bringing sinners home to God. However, Hooker requires careful handling because “preparationist” theology can be misunderstood or overapplied.

## 9. New article modules unlocked by v54

### Module A — The heart after prayer

The heart is revealed not only before prayer, when it asks, but after prayer, when it waits, watches, forgets, resents, submits, or rejoices. A man may pray with correct words and then live as though no answer should be watched for; or he may learn to interpret delay not as abandonment, but as a summons to trust.

### Module B — Faith and love are not invisible abstractions

Faith and love are set upon Christ as their object, but they do not remain bodiless. They appear in good works, speech, endurance, prayer, forgiveness, and costly obedience. Therefore the series must not separate heart-depth from outward practice.

### Module C — Christ draws the troubled heart

A series about the heart must not leave the reader alone with the anatomy of his sin. The crucified Christ is not only the judge of the heart’s false lovers; He is the One who draws weak, troubled, and sinful people to Himself.

### Module D — Doctrine pressed into the heart

Catechesis is not dead information. Right doctrine should become worship, fear of God, humility, faith, hope, prayer, and obedience. If doctrine stays outside the heart, it can even become material for religious self-protection.
