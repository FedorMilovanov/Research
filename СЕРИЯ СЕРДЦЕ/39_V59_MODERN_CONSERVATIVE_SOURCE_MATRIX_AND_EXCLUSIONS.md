# V59 — MODERN CONSERVATIVE SOURCE MATRIX / EXCLUSIONS / WEB DEEPENING

**Purpose:** continue the research without expanding the archive into hundreds of files. This layer does not add a new theological theme first; it adds a stricter conservative screening grid for modern helpers and a clearer exclusion policy for sources that weaken Scripture, sin, repentance, regeneration, or Christ-centered sanctification.

**Core rule:** Scripture, confessions, Reformers and Puritans remain the doctrinal backbone. Modern sources may clarify, summarize, or pastorally apply; they must not replace the primary theological frame.

## 2. Conservative source hierarchy for writing the series

### Tier 1 — doctrinal anchors

Use these as binding framework for the article:

1. Scripture.
2. 1689 London Baptist Confession / Westminster where appropriate and clearly marked.
3. Calvin, Reformed orthodox, Puritans.
4. Baptist confessional sources such as Founders / 1689 materials.

**Usage:** may anchor doctrine of Scripture, sin, heart, repentance, faith, sanctification, church, assurance, perseverance, providence, law/gospel.

### Tier 2 — strong conservative modern helpers

Use these to support, clarify, and apply:

- TMS / MacArthur / Grace Church / Master’s Seminary Journal.
- ACBC / BCC / CCEF when kept under Scripture and not used as psychology-first authority.
- 9Marks, Ligonier, RTS, WTS, PRTS, SBTS, Founders, G3.
- Packer, Carson, Beeke, Piper / Desiring God, with author-specific caution.

**Usage:** may support conservative framing, but cite specific author/material, not the platform in general.

### Tier 3 — yellow caution modern evangelical sources

- TGC broadly: use Carson or specific author, not “TGC says.”
- Packer: useful on Scripture, knowing God, Puritan spirituality; not a Baptist confessional anchor.
- Bonhoeffer: only illustrative for community / confession / isolation; not doctrinal backbone.
- BibleProject and broad evangelical media: useful for accessible summaries only after doctrinal screening; not primary exegesis for a sensitive series.

### Tier 4 — secular auxiliary only

CDC, WHO, NIMH, SAMHSA, NCTSN, NASEM, PubMed habit research and trauma materials.

**Allowed:** safety, suicide risk, abuse/IPV, trauma-informed caution, medical/psychological terminology, habit formation data.

**Forbidden:** defining sin, repentance, regeneration, holiness, assurance, spiritual bondage, pastoral discipline, or doctrine of the heart.

### Tier 5 — excluded as doctrinal sources

Any author, ministry, academic frame, or article that denies or relativizes:

- inerrancy / truthfulness / authority / sufficiency of Scripture;
- uniqueness of Christ;
- necessity of repentance and faith;
- moral reality of sin before God;
- regeneration / new birth;
- biblical sexual ethics;
- final judgment;
- supernatural work of the Spirit;
- sin as guilt, corruption, rebellion, and bondage, not merely trauma/social conditioning.

Such sources may only appear in a contrast note if there is a specific reason.

## 3. New web-checked additions and refinements

### 3.1 9Marks — useful conservative church/practice layer

9Marks is useful for church, preaching, discipline, sufficiency of Scripture, membership, and pastoral practice. It should be used as a modern ecclesial/pastoral helper, not as a confessional authority above Scripture or the 1689.

**Best use in the series:**

- local church as means of heart care;
- church discipline and restoration;
- sufficiency and clarity of Scripture;
- preaching and membership as contexts where the heart is formed and corrected.

**Risk:** their authors vary; cite the exact author/article, not the platform generically.

### 3.2 Grace Church / Grace to You / MacArthur — strong conservative but not confessional Baptist

Grace Church’s doctrinal statement strongly affirms verbal inspiration and the authority of the 66 books. MacArthur/GTY are useful on inerrancy, exposition, lordship, sanctification, and Scripture authority.

**Best use:**

- inerrancy and expository ministry;
- Scripture as authority over heart diagnosis;
- pastoral warnings against psychology replacing Scripture;
- practical holiness and lordship of Christ.

**Risk:** not 1689 confessional Baptist; dispensational distinctives should not be imported into Baptist/Reformed confessional claims without need.

### 3.3 ACBC / BCC / CCEF — biblical counseling layer

ACBC and BCC give strong statements of Scripture’s sufficiency for counseling. CCEF is also usable, especially where it balances sinners/sufferers, Scripture, wisdom, pastoral gentleness, and careful attention to suffering.

**Best use:**

- counseling as personal ministry of the Word;
- sin + suffering balance;
- sufficiency of Scripture without anti-medical simplification;
- pastoral triage and safety boundaries.

**Risk:** do not flatten all biblical counseling streams into one voice. For article writing, name the exact institution/author and their function.

### 3.4 Desiring God / Piper — useful but author-specific

Desiring God is generally conservative on inerrancy and Scripture, with useful resources on faith, desire, joy, worship, and Christian hedonism.

**Best use:**

- desire, joy, worship, faith, treasuring Christ;
- walking by faith and obedience of faith;
- Bible’s truthfulness.

**Risk:** not a confessional Baptist anchor for the project; use concrete materials and verify claims.

### 3.5 G3 — conservative auxiliary

G3 is usable for authority/sufficiency of Scripture and Reformed/Baptist-adjacent conservative evangelical framing.

**Best use:** quick conservative summary, not primary proof.

### 3.6 Red-flag example: anti-inerrancy / inerrancy-problematic sources

Materials that call inerrancy “problematic” or explicitly argue against it are not acceptable as theological anchors for the article. They can only appear in a warning/contrast note if needed.

## 5. Practical writing rules after V59

1. Do not write: “modern scholarship says...” without specifying source and theological status.
2. Do not cite platforms as if they are persons: write “Wayne Mack in TMSJ,” not “TMS says,” when discussing a specific claim.
3. Do not use secular sources to define sin, repentance, or sanctification.
4. Use secular sources only to protect people from pastoral negligence in safety/medical/trauma contexts.
5. Do not let biblical counseling sources become an independent magisterium; they serve Scripture.
6. Prefer Puritan/Reformed primary texts for the core spiritual anatomy of the heart.
7. Use modern sources as guards, summaries, and pastoral bridges.
8. Quote exactly or summarize plainly without quotation marks.

## 6. New article-use modules from V59

### Module A — Why Scripture must govern the diagnosis of the heart

The human heart cannot finally interpret itself. Because the heart is self-deceiving, a theology of the heart must be ruled by Scripture, not by therapeutic categories, cultural plausibility, or personal feeling. Modern conservative sources can help clarify the doctrine of Scripture, but the article’s frame must rest on the Bible and confessional theology.

### Module B — Sinners and sufferers without liberal reductionism

The series should preserve both realities: people sin before God, and people suffer under bodily weakness, trauma, oppression, abuse, and the groaning of a fallen world. Liberal reductionism tends to dissolve sin into social/therapeutic categories; harsh moralism tends to erase suffering. A conservative biblical approach must reject both.

### Module C — Counseling as personal ministry of the Word, not therapy baptized with verses

Biblical counseling must not become mere psychological technique with Bible verses attached. It must be Christ-centered, Scripture-governed, church-aware, and wise about limits. It may use extra-biblical observations, but those observations must never become final authority over the soul.

### Module D — Conservative modern sources cannot replace Puritan depth

TMS, BCC, ACBC, CCEF, 9Marks, Ligonier, PRTS, RTS, WTS, and similar sources are helpful modern gatekeepers. But the deep anatomy of the heart in this research still belongs primarily to Scripture, Calvin, the confessions, Owen, Charnock, Boston, Edwards, Sibbes, Goodwin, Brooks, Baxter, Perkins, Burgess, Flavel, Ames, and related primary sources.

## 7. Red-list rules for future web search

Reject as doctrinal anchors any source that:

- explicitly opposes inerrancy or treats Scripture as errant religious witness;
- treats biblical sexual ethics as outdated/oppressive;
- denies penal substitution, wrath, judgment, repentance, or regeneration;
- treats Christ as one religious figure among many;
- reduces sin to trauma, biology, oppression, or ignorance only;
- treats sanctification as self-actualization rather than union with Christ and work of the Spirit;
- mocks or relativizes conservative Protestant confessions.
